The History of The Internet

The Internet got its start in the United States more than 50 years ago as a government weapon in the cold war. The United States department of defense had recently created the Advanced Research Projects Agency (ARPA), to keep its tech ahead of the soviets.

A computer enthusiast named Jospeh Licklider helped convince ARPA to fund research into a computer network connecting scientists and engineers thought the country.

A few colleges agreed to be involved and ARPA started building the network in 1969, they called it the ARPANET. It started fairly small, as a sort of messaging service between computer at University of California, Stanford University, and The University of Utah

ARPANET kept growing through the 1970s and had a network of over 100 computers by 1978. Other networks were popping up around the world. Transmission Control was invented in order for all networks to communicate to each other, forming what became known as The Internet!

By the end of 1987, over 20,000 computers were already connected. However, The Internet was still restricted to universities and corporations which use it for specific types of collaboration and research.

In the early 1990s, connecting to The Internet was slow. Your computer had to place a phone call to the network through a modem, which translated between the digital signals used in computers and analog signals used in land line phones.

The Web was made public in 1993. It was the final major piece of today's Internet was in place. With the Web and Internet finally paired up and publicly accessible, the number of computers using the internet the 1990s.
